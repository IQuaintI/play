Introduction

Learning land navigation seems like a strange thing to do in a world where we have the entirety of the world only a few taps a way, but there is still something to be said for the old-school ways of doing things.

Don't worry. Maps rading may seem difficult but maps are nothing more than a graphic representation of the Earth's surface drawn to scale from an overhead view. This techinical document will go over some of the basics of land navigation.

  • “God created war so that Americans would learn geography.” ― Mark Twain
    • What you need

    The only things you need to be succesful at land navigation is a map, a compass, a protracter, a writing utensil, and some luck.

    If you want any more information on land navigation Army Field Manual/Training Circular (FM/TC) 3-25.26 covers everything you will ever need to know regarding the matter.

  • “By failing to prepare, you are preparing to fail.” ― Benjamin Franklin
    • Red

    Ever wonder what all those colors on a map mean? Well have no fear as the following will hopefully go answer every question you had on the matter.

    Red represents cultural features such as populated areas, roads, and boundaries. However, this color is only found on older maps as it has been superseded by the red-brown color.

  • “The spirit of arrogance most definitely makes you shine. It paints a bright red target on your own forehead.” ― Criss Jami
    • Brown

    Brown is next up on our color wheel and it represents all relief features (this is just a fancy way of saying terrain) and differences in elevation through contour lines. It also shows cuitvated land on when using a red-light readable map.

    This color is also used more so on older maps.

  • “The color brown, I realized, is anything but nondescript. It comes in as many hues as there are colors of earth, with is commonly presumed infinite.” ― Barbara Kingsolver
    • Red-Brown

    A mixture of two colors that exist to identify cultural features (human-made features), relief features, non-surveyed spot elavations, and surveyed elavation.

    Can be seen on red-light friendly maps

  • “Do I not destroy my enemies when I make them my friends?” ― Abraham Lincoln
    • Green

    Makes note of vegetation that carries military significance such as woods, orchards, and vineyards.

    Maps tend to have a lot of this.

  • “If a tree falls in the forest and nobody is there to hear it, doesn't it just lie there and rot?” ― Chuck Palahniuk
    • Blue

    This color represents hydrography (measuring the physical features of water) or other water features such as lakes, swamps, rivers, and drainage.

    Note that while maps can show how big a body of water is, it does not accurately portray depth.
